Most teams treat Conversion Rate Optimization like a slot machine. They swap a button from blue to green, ship it, and wonder why revenue didn't move. When it doesn't, they blame the audience.

I ran 50+ A/B tests at Optum across a stretch of years — the ones that worked averaged a 47% engagement uplift, and plenty of them lost. What separated the winners from the noise was never a clever tactic. It was the process underneath. CRO isn't guesswork or trend-chasing. It's a repeatable loop for understanding how people actually behave and then removing whatever is standing between them and the thing they already came to do.

The loop has four parts: **discover, optimize, analyze, repeat.** Here's how I run it.

## Step 1: Discovery — the detective work

You can't fix a leak until you know where the water is coming from. Discovery is where you gather the evidence that becomes a hypothesis. I work it in four passes.

**Technical first, because broken beats clever.** Before I think about psychology, I confirm the site actually works. If a page takes six seconds to load on a mid-range Android, no headline is going to save it. Check device and browser reports — does it quietly fall apart on Safari? Check load time. And check that "mobile-first" means a real mobile experience, not a desktop layout someone shrank and shipped.

**Then a heuristic walkthrough.** I audit the page by hand through four lenses:

- **Friction** — where is the product making someone work too hard? Long forms, ambiguous navigation, a checkout that asks for things it doesn't need yet.
- **Distraction** — is an oversized banner or an unprompted pop-up pulling attention off the primary action?
- **Relevance** — does the page match why the person came? I use the seven-second test: a stranger lands cold, and within seven seconds they should be able to tell you what this is and who it's for. If they can't, nothing below the fold matters.
- **Motivation** — why should they care? The layout has to speak to what this specific person actually wants, not what we wish they wanted.

**Then the hard numbers.** This is where the money leaks show up. But before trusting any of it: is the tracking even right? If your tags are broken, your data isn't neutral — it's actively lying to you, and confidently. I've watched teams optimize for months against a number that was wrong from day one. Once the data is trustworthy, I look at the baseline — landing pages, sessions, bounce, transactions, conversion rate — then where the good traffic comes from, then the funnel itself. When 80% of users abandon at the shipping step, you don't need a survey to know where to look next.

**Then the "why."** Analytics tells you *what* happened; it almost never tells you *why*. So I watch real sessions — recordings, scroll maps, click maps. People click things that aren't links. They scroll straight past the CTA you spent a week on. And I go to the source directly: surveys, a handful of one-on-one interviews, support tickets, the way competitors frame the same offer. The quantitative data finds the room that's on fire. The qualitative data tells you who lit the match.

## Step 2: Optimize — the part where you say no

By the end of discovery you'll have a list of fifty things you *could* fix. The skill in CRO is not generating ideas — it's choosing.

Don't grab the easiest ticket. Score the list. I use ICE — Impact, Confidence, Ease — so the decision is "how much lift, for how much engineering time," not "what can I ship by Friday." It also forces you to write down your confidence, which is humbling in the best way.

Then you build the variation and let it run. The original is the **control**, the new version is the **variation**, and real behavior — not the loudest person in the room — picks the winner. At Optum, the credit-card application funnel was where this mattered most: small, well-reasoned changes to a high-intent flow compound into real revenue, and the only honest way to know which ones work is to test them against each other.

## Step 3: Analyze — face the result, not the one you wanted

When the data comes in, the first question is whether the result is real — statistical significance before celebration.

If the variation won, roll it to 100% and put a number on the lift. That number is how CRO earns its budget.

If it lost or tied, that's not a failure — it's an entry in your library. A losing test tells you what *doesn't* move this audience, which quietly saves you from a dozen worse decisions later. The teams that get good at this are the ones that stop being embarrassed by losing tests and start mining them.

## Step 4: Repeat — it's a process, not a project

Here's the part most people skip. The winning variation isn't the finish line; it's the new baseline. You take what you just learned, feed it back into discovery, and go find the next bottleneck.

Done once, CRO is a stunt. Done as a loop, the micro-wins compound — and a year of small, evidenced changes moves the bottom line more than any single redesign ever has for me.

## The actual point

CRO isn't about manipulating anyone into buying. It's about making it as effortless as possible for people to do the thing they already came to do. Stop guessing, dig into the data, and let your users show you how they want to buy.
